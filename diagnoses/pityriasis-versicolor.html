Pityriasis Versicolor

Superficial yeast-related discoloration on trunk skin

Sacroiliitis Hip FAI Right Knee Lumbar Spine Telogen Effluvium Pityriasis Versicolor ENT / Rhinitis Incidental

Overview

Pityriasis versicolor is a common superficial yeast overgrowth that changes skin pigmentation and can cause fine scale or mild itch.

Possible causes: Malassezia yeast that normally lives on the skin; Heat, humidity, sweating, and oily skin; Recurrence-prone skin environment.

Report source: 7102750361.pdf.

Key Findings

  • Red varicolored macules on the inframammary region and back.
  • Diagnosis recorded as preliminary.
  • Typical location for pityriasis versicolor.

Symptoms

  • Light, dark, pink, copper, or red-brown patches.
  • Fine scale.
  • Mild itch or no symptoms.
  • Color contrast can become more visible after sun exposure.

Impact

Short term

Itching or cosmetic concern; usually not medically dangerous.

Long term

Recurrence is common in warm or humid conditions, but prevention can reduce flares.

If ignored

Patches may spread or persist, and pigment changes can remain noticeable even after active yeast clears.

Treatment Plan

  1. 1Confirm with dermatology if uncertain, often by exam or KOH scraping.
  2. 2Use topical selenium sulfide or azole shampoo/cream as directed.
  3. 3Expect pigment normalization to lag behind yeast clearance.
  4. 4Consider preventive monthly topical use if recurrence is frequent.
  5. 5Reserve oral antifungals for extensive or recurrent cases under clinician supervision.

Urgent warning signs

Not usually urgent; seek care if rash is painful, rapidly spreading, blistering, infected, or diagnosis is uncertain.
